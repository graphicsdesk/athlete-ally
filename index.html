Ivy League athletics lead the nation in LGBTQ inclusion. Meet the athletes and coaches leading the way.

By Rebecca Wachen

October 3rd, 2022

Photo by Violet Mendelsund

Only 15 NCAA Division I institutions initially received a perfect score on Athlete Ally’s Athlete Equality Index, a measure that assesses LGBTQ inclusion policies and practices within each school’s athletic department. Of the 15 schools who received perfect scores in the August 2022 report, three of them participate in the Ivy League, the most of any conference.

Athlete Ally, an organization that works to end homophobia and transphobia in college athletics, created the AEI to assess each athletic department’s LGBTQ inclusion policies and practices to promote conversation and collaboration within the departments. The AEI assesses each school’s nondiscrimination statement, transgender inclusion policy, sexual harassment policy, fan code of conduct, partnerships with the school’s LGBTQ centers, LGBTQ educational resources, and pro-LGBTQ trainings for athletes and staff to determine each school’s score.

Columbia and Penn currently hold the Ivy League’s highest scores, each earning a score of 100 out of 100. Additionally, the Ivy League topped all athletic conferences with an average score of 77.5 out of 100. While Dartmouth was initially included as a perfect-scoring institution in Athlete Ally’s August 2022 report, it currently has a score of 85.

Four of the eight Ivy League institutions have an active Athlete Ally chapter on their campus, which has allowed LGBTQ students and allies to come together to create more inclusive athletic spaces on campus. The chapter for Columbia and Barnard is the only chapter to receive a perfect score and have an active organization on campus and will be honored at the 2022 Athlete Ally Action Awards on Oct. 25.

Columbia and Barnard’s Athlete Ally chapter was started in 2020 by women’s rowing team members Honor Clements, CC ’21, and Ruba Nadar, BC ’21. Under Clements and Nadar, as well as current senior co-presidents Jane Collins and Cameron Nelson, the club has grown rapidly over the past two years. It has worked with the Columbia athletic department to organize virtual 5k runs, webinars on athletic inclusivity, Pride Night with the women’s basketball team, and much more. This year, the club hopes to have a representative from every Columbia team join the chapter.

“Having an Athlete Ally chapter on campus and seeing it grow into a community has allowed us to be more comfortable with our identities on the teams we love,'” Collins said upon accepting the Action Award. “It was the leadership of our Athlete Ally Chapter founders that allowed me to embrace my identity as a queer woman, and I hope that by increasing visibility, we can help others feel more comfortable as well.”

Even for the Ivy League schools without a chapter on campus, the Ancient Eight stands out from other athletic conferences when it comes to LGBTQ inclusion on campus. While only about a quarter of schools have accessible nondiscriminatory and sexual misconduct policies, every Ivy League school has this information readily available. Additionally, while only 12 percent of college athletic departments have a publicly accessible trans inclusion policy, six of the eight Ivy League schools have this information available, with Brown and Dartmouth as the exceptions.

While the eight Ivy League schools’ athletic departments have provided support and information for LGBTQ athletes, it has often been the athletes themselves who are able to raise awareness and combat homophobia in their own respective teams.

Mason Darrow, an offensive lineman on Princeton’s football team who graduated in 2017, hid the fact that he was gay for some time, partly because he was unsure if he would be accepted by his teammates. He felt trapped, he told Bleacher Report. But once he found the courage to come out to his teammates, he considered it the best decision he ever made. Becoming the first openly gay college football player, Darrow inspired other gay football players to come out after him—and be accepted by their teams.

It took three years for Dartmouth tennis player John Speicher to come out to his college teammates. It was something he contemplated throughout his time at college, and his fear of ostracization caused him to doubt himself on the court. Speicher found the confidence to come out in 2020 on National Coming Out Day on his Instagram. Despite his anxieties, he received an overwhelmingly positive response from friends, family, and teammates. Although the pandemic stopped Speicher from playing tennis at Dartmouth after coming out, he still hopes to increase the visibility of gay athletes, especially in men’s tennis. There are no openly gay men on the current ATP tour, and Speicher hopes that increased representation in the future will give more tennis players the strength to come out.

When rower Gus Hirschfeld was named a captain of Brown’s men’s crew team, he felt he needed to come out to his teammates—which he did on the team’s first night back on campus before classes. Through his experience, he wrote for OutSports, his teammates began to understand allyship, and he discovered the importance of community in supporting people to come out as their true selves.

In addition, in recent years, several Ivy League swimmers have come out as transgender. Harvard’s Schuyler Bailar was the first openly transgender Division I swimmer, and since then, several other competitive swimmers have come out as transgender. Yale’s Iszac Henig and Penn’s Lia Thomas are two more Ivy League transgender swimmers who have come out and competed in recent years.

On the coaching end, Columbia lightweight rowing head coach Nich Lee Parker, TC ’20, is the first openly gay head coach of a men’s Division I team. Parker has led his team to much success, guiding the Lions to two national titles and earning Ivy League Coach of the Year honors in 2018.

“I think that symbiotic [coaching] relationship is beautiful in its own right, but it was made even more special because of one simple fact: Of the hundreds of men’s D1 head coaches out there in every sport, I’m the only one that is openly gay … and these guys simply didn’t care. They wanted to be the best and they trusted a gay coach to take them there,” Parker told Outsports in 2018.

Organizations like Athlete Ally have worked to create a more inclusive environment in sports for LGBTQ athletes and coaches. With Division I institutions leading the way with their LGBTQ inclusion policies, activists are hopeful that other conferences will follow to make collegiate athletics more inclusive for all.
